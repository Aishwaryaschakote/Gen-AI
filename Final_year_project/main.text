% ABSTRACT
% -------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
WellNest is an AI-powered nutrition and fitness assistant designed to simplify and improve the accuracy of daily diet tracking. Traditional calorie-tracking apps require users to manually search for food items, estimate portion sizes, and input nutritional values, which is time-consuming and often inaccurate. As a result, users frequently lose motivation and fail to track their meals consistently, limiting long-term progress toward their health goals.

WellNest addresses these challenges using advanced AI vision models such as OpenAI and Google Gemini. With a single photo of a meal, the system automatically identifies multiple food items, estimates portion sizes, and calculates calorie and nutrient values. This significantly reduces manual effort and increases the reliability of tracking. The app also collects key user details—including age, height, weight, gender, activity level, and health conditions—to generate personalized nutrition targets tailored to individual needs.


Built with FlutterFlow, the app offers a clean and intuitive user interface suitable for users of all ages. Supabase powers the backend, ensuring secure authentication, real-time database operations, and seamless storage of user profiles and meal logs. In addition to automated meal analysis, WellNest provides personalized diet recommendations, progress insights, and easy-to-understand visual data to help users stay consistent and make informed choices.

By combining AI automation with personalized health guidance, WellNest enables users to build sustainable, healthier habits with minimal effort and maximum accuracy.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background}

In reality, living well means eating right while staying active - habits shaped by everyday decisions and routines. As conditions like obesity, diabetes, high blood pressure, or heart issues grow from lifestyle factors, folks pay closer attention to what they eat, aiming for thoughtful meal plans. Still, even when aware, most struggle with knowing enough about nutrients, lacking hours in the day, or just failing to stick with monitoring meals regularly.

People often log meals by looking up foods, guessing serving amounts, maybe typing calories into a phone or notebook. That way takes forever, feels like work, also breaks down when mistakes happen. Since most folks get portions wrong - writing stuff late or not at all - the records end up messy or missing bits. Weeks go by, annoyance builds, tracking gets ignored more, then stops altogether.

Mobile tech’s moving fast, so folks now want health apps that act more like smart helpers than basic tools - ones that can actually learn your habits. Instead of just listing meals, imagine an app spotting what you eat from a photo, thanks to clever image software. It doesn’t stop there - it guesses how much you’re eating, then breaks down calories and nutrients on the spot. No more typing every bite; everything happens instantly in the background. This kind of system turns meal tracking into something way smoother and less annoying.

In fact, when you mix this smart tech with learning algorithms or online data tools, people start seeing real-time feedback on what they eat, tips that match their personal targets, while also grasping how small habits shape overall well-being. These setups keep things steady, reduce uncertainty, helping folks choose meals based on clear info instead of guesses. Linking artificial intelligence to common health apps fills the missing piece between what users actually want and the old-school tracking methods falling short - so staying healthy becomes easier, smoother, doable for everyone.


\section{Problems with Manual Calorie Logging}

Manual calorie logging presents several challenges, which reduce accuracy and make nutrition tracking inefficient. Some of the major issues include:

\begin{itemize}

    \item For every food item, the user must manually search and enter its calorie value, which becomes cumbersome—especially when logging multiple meals per day. This repetitive effort often discourages long-term use of nutrition-tracking applications.

    \item Serving sizes are frequently misestimated because most people are not trained to gauge portion sizes or gram measurements accurately. Even small errors in estimation can cause large variations in total calorie counts for the day.

    \item Logging meals becomes monotonous and labor-intensive, particularly for individuals who consume home-cooked or ethnic foods that may not exist in standard nutrition databases. This slows down the process and reduces user motivation over time.

    \item Users often forget to record meals, especially snacks or beverages consumed throughout the day. Such missed entries lead to incomplete and unreliable nutrition data, affecting long-term accuracy.

    \item Complex or mixed dishes are difficult to log manually because they contain multiple ingredients prepared together (e.g., biryani, pav bhaji, dosa with chutney). Estimating calories for such dishes requires breaking them into individual components, which is impractical for most users.

    \item Manual logging heavily depends on user discipline and memory, making the process prone to human errors such as approximations, incorrect database selections, or inconsistent logging habits.

    \item Calorie values vary significantly based on cooking methods (fried, grilled, boiled, etc.), but manual logging rarely accounts for these differences, resulting in inaccurate dietary assessments.

    \item Users with busy schedules struggle to log meals regularly, causing gaps in the dietary history and reducing the effectiveness of long-term health tracking.

    \item Manual tracking does not provide real-time feedback, as users only receive results after submitting entries. This makes it less useful for immediate decision-making during meals.

\end{itemize}


\section{Role of AI in Nutrition and Fitness}

AI changed how we handle food and workouts by making it possible to study diets in ways that just weren't doable before with pen-and-paper logs. Today’s smart tools don’t stop at counting calories - they use image recognition along with pattern detection plus custom feedback to give a full picture of your well-being.

Take NutriTrack AI - this kind of smart camera system spots different foods on one dish, even if they’re stacked or crooked. It cuts down how much people have to step in, so less tapping and logging every time. These systems run on advanced learning setups that study details like color, surface feel, form, or where things sit on the plate. That helps them make sense of messy combos - think spicy stews, packed bowls, Indian thalis, or anything with lots of parts.

Besides spotting objects, AI uses depth guesses along with split-up image parts to roughly measure food amounts - boosting how close estimates get to reality. That helps fix a major flaw in standard calorie counting: wrong size assumptions messing up diet records. By cutting out the uncertainty, AI gives quick answers that match actual nutrition way better.

Advanced machine learning doesn't just spot food - it matches what it sees with massive nutrition libraries to figure out calories, macros, and vitamins. Instead of guessing, this method uses live detection alongside trusted nutrient info, so results are way more reliable. That means regular people get better insights, whether they're trying to lose fat, build muscle, or follow a medical diet plan.

AI’s great at tailoring things to you. By checking how users act, what they eat, past records, daily movement, plus their health details, smart systems offer custom food plans, portion tweaks, or better snack picks. Over time, suggestions improve - thanks to ongoing learning - so the whole thing fits your life closer as days go by.

AI-powered eating tools keep adjusting based on your habits, so you stay ahead instead of fixing things after the fact. No need to wait days to see how well you're doing - smart tech gives instant tips right when you eat. That helps build steady habits over time, which is usually the hardest part of sticking to better food choices. Think of it as a digital buddy that tracks meals, checks nutrients, and offers custom advice without manual work. It connects expert-backed diet rules with real-life routines, helping people young and old make smarter picks effortlessly.

\chapter{Literature Work}

\section{Reviewed Research Papers}

A close look at earlier studies helped figure out what’s new in AI for spotting foods, analyzing nutrients, and giving custom eating suggestions. Work from the last ten years reveals big steps forward in using deep learning to name dishes, create meal ideas, also track diets with smart tools. Several papers show how well CNNs work alongside transformers, plus YOLO setups and large language networks when naming meals, building healthy menus, or guessing what users might like.
Still, studies show ongoing issues. Many tools fail at identifying combo meals, global flavors, serving sizes, plus changes in light or how food’s arranged. In a similar way, diet suggesters work well for custom menus yet need clear data from people instead of using photo-based tracking. Medical-grade devices give solid results but don’t fit daily use easily; meanwhile, common phone apps lean on hand-entered logs and offer shaky nutrition feedback.
Put together, the analysis points out something missing - right now, there’s no tool that brings food recognition by AI, precise nutrition guessing, automatic tracking, or custom eating tips all into one easy-to-use app. That missing piece is exactly why NutriTrack AI was created.

\subsection{Khamesian et al. [1]} proposed \textit{NutriGen}, an LLM-powered personalized meal planning system that integrates user constraints, nutrition databases, and prompt engineering to automatically generate weekly diet plans. Their study reported very low caloric prediction errors (1.55--3.68\%), outperforming traditional dietary recommendation methods. \textbf{However, the system does not support image-based food detection or real-time calorie estimation.}




\subsection{Dimitropoulos et al. [2]} introduced a deep generative meal recommendation model using a VAE and RNN architecture, aligned with WHO and EFSA nutritional guidelines. By integrating ChatGPT for enhancing meal diversity, the system achieved superior performance across more than 4,000 synthetic and real user profiles. \textbf{However, it does not perform automated food recognition or portion-size estimation from images.}

\subsection{Moyen et al. [3]} reviewed AI-powered nutrition apps such as FRANI, CALO Mama Plus, Keenoa, and Heali, demonstrating improvements in diet adherence, weight control, and glucose monitoring in clinical trials. \textbf{However, these applications still show inconsistencies in nutrient estimation, especially for mixed dishes.}

\subsection{Mokdara et al. [4]} developed a deep neural network–based personalized food recommendation system that models user eating patterns effectively. \textbf{However, the model does not conduct nutritional analysis or handle multi-item meals, limiting its usefulness for health-focused applications.}

\subsection{Rostami et al. [5]} presented an explainable food recommender using deep image clustering and community detection to improve recommendation interpretability and visual-similarity accuracy. \textbf{However, it lacks nutrient estimation and portion-size prediction capabilities.}

\subsection{Iwendi et al. [6]} proposed an IoMT-assisted dietary recommendation framework using machine learning and LSTM models for clinical diet planning. \textbf{However, despite strong clinical performance, it is not scalable or practical for everyday consumer use.}

\subsection{Zhang et al. [7]} introduced a sequence-based personalized food recommendation approach combining LSTM with collaborative filtering to predict user food choices. \textbf{However, the system does not evaluate nutritional suitability or support mixed-dish recognition.}

\subsection{Jung and Chung [8]} designed a knowledge-based dietary recommendation model using expert rules and collaborative filtering for obesity management. \textbf{However, the system struggles with scalability and lacks real-time automated nutrition tracking.}

\subsection{Ilukpitiya et al. [9]} proposed an AI-driven personalized fitness and nutrition system that uses CNNs, YOLOv8, and Random Forest classifiers to identify somatotypes and generate adaptive workout routines. \textbf{However, the system focuses primarily on fitness and does not incorporate image-based food detection or automatic calorie tracking.}

\subsection{Sah et al. [10]} reviewed AI applications in HIV management, highlighting the role of machine learning, deep learning, and NLP in diagnosis, drug-resistance prediction, treatment personalization, and patient monitoring. \textbf{However, the review is disease-specific and does not address daily nutrition or general wellness applications.}

\subsection{Papastratis et al. [11]} developed a hybrid nutrition recommendation model combining a VAE, a GRU network, and ChatGPT to generate weekly balanced meal plans. Evaluations across 3,000 virtual and 1,000 real profiles showed significant improvements in accuracy, personalization, and safety. \textbf{However, the system lacks support for image-based food detection and real-time nutrient extraction.}

\subsection{Antonelli and Donelli [12]} conducted a narrative review of AI-powered precision nutrition apps such as Keenoa, FRANI, CALO Mama Plus, and Heali, concluding that AI significantly improves tracking accuracy, dietary adherence, and weight-loss outcomes. \textbf{However, the review highlights persistent limitations such as low personalization depth, unreliable nutrient predictions, and privacy-related concerns.}
 

\section*{Summary}

Most current research shows steps forward in AI-driven meal suggestions and diet plans, but issues still remain. While some tools can spot single foods well, they often fail when meals contain multiple ingredients. Figuring out serving sizes is tricky, plus pulling exact nutritional details automatically doesn’t work smoothly yet. A lot depend on people typing what they eat, which makes tailoring advice to medical needs or fitness targets weak. Systems used in healthcare settings don't fit regular users’ daily lives, whereas consumer apps vary wildly in data quality and leave privacy exposed. AI tools for workouts often give custom routines yet skip meal and calorie monitoring. Still, research points toward building a single smart system - linking food detection, nutrient guesses, plus tailored eating and exercise advice - all in one place.

\chapter{Drawbacks of Existing Work and Objectives}

\section{Drawbacks}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Current AI systems struggle to identify combo plates or meals containing multiple food items, making calorie estimation difficult and often inaccurate.
    
    \item Most tools fail to estimate portion sizes reliably, which directly affects the accuracy of nutrient calculations.
    
    \item Popular diet-tracking applications require users to enter food items manually, a process that is time-consuming and prone to errors.
    
    \item Personalization is limited in existing tools; many systems do not take into account user-specific health conditions, goals, or cultural food habits.
    
    \item Several research studies focus only on providing recommendations rather than offering real-time nutrient analysis from food images.
    
    \item Clinical and IoMT-based nutrition systems do not scale well and are impractical for daily consumer use.
    
    \item Many AI-powered fitness applications do not include meal logging or calorie tracking, resulting in a fragmented user experience.
    
    \item Existing food databases predominantly cover Western cuisines, reducing accuracy for Indian or other culturally diverse food items.
    
    \item No current solution provides a fully automated, end-to-end workflow that integrates food detection, portion estimation, nutrition breakdown, and personalized diet–exercise recommendations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Overview of Processing Pipeline}
% -------------------------------------------------

The Generative AI healthcare system follows a structured end-to-end pipeline:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The user enters personal health details and sets goals.
    \item The system computes metabolic values such as BMR and TDEE.
    \item The user uploads a food image or daily activity input.
    \item The AI Vision model identifies food items and estimates portion sizes.
    \item Detected items are matched with a nutrition database.
    \item Calories, macronutrients, and health indicators are computed automatically.
    \item Logs are stored in Supabase and daily summaries are updated.
    \item Personalized AI-driven health recommendations are generated for the user.
\end{itemize}

Each component of the pipeline works together to create an automated  
AI-powered healthcare monitoring ecosystem.

% -------------------------------------------------
\section{User Health Calculation Algorithms}
% -------------------------------------------------

User metabolic rates and energy needs are calculated using  
clinically validated formulas.

\subsection*{BMR (Mifflin–St Jeor Formula)}

\[
BMR =
\begin{cases}
10W + 6.25H - 5A + 5, & \text{Male} \\
10W + 6.25H - 5A - 161, & \text{Female}
\end{cases}
\]

\subsection*{TDEE Calculation}

\[
TDEE = BMR \times ActivityFactor
\]

\subsection*{Pseudocode: Health Calculation}
\begin{verbatim}
Input: weight, height, age, gender, activity_level
If gender = male:
    BMR = 10*weight + 6.25*height - 5*age + 5
Else:
    BMR = 10*weight + 6.25*height - 5*age - 161
TDEE = BMR * activity_level
Return BMR, TDEE
\end{verbatim}

\section{AI Vision-Based Food Analysis Pipeline}
% -------------------------------------------------

The system uses advanced Generative AI models (OpenAI/Gemini)  
to analyze food images, detect meal components, and estimate nutrition.

\subsection*{Pseudocode: AI Vision Algorithm}
\begin{verbatim}
Input: Food Image
Convert image to Base64
Create API request with:
    - input = Base64 image
    - model = "OpenAI Vision"
Send request to API
Receive detected food items and bounding boxes
If multiple items:
    Process each item individually
Estimate portion size for each item
Extract:
    - food_items
    - portion_sizes
    - calories
Store results in Supabase DB
Match items to nutrition dataset
Generate JSON with macros + calories
Return nutrition JSON
\end{verbatim}

practical, user-focused health guidance. The results highlight the potential

\section{Discussion}

The outcome of the system evaluation shows promising performance across  
multiple dimensions:

\subsection*{1. Performance of AI Vision Model}

The AI-based detection model was tested on diverse food categories such as:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Indian thali meals
    \item South Indian dishes
    \item North Indian curries
    \item Continental dishes
    \item Fast food items
\end{itemize}

The system achieved reliable performance in identifying food components  
even when plates contained mixtures of ingredients or items overlapping  
each other. Traditional systems often fail in distinguishing such  
mixed dishes, but the generative model handled them with significantly  
better consistency.

Minor inaccuracies were noticed in the following cases:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Dishes with heavy gravies (e.g., sambar, dal, korma)
    \item Purees or blended foods
    \item Low-light or blurry images
\end{itemize}

However, repeated tests showed that these errors had minimal impact  
on overall calorie estimation because portion sizes remained within  
a stable error margin.

\subsection*{2. Accuracy of Nutritional Estimation}

The calorie and macronutrient estimates were compared against  
standard benchmarks such as:

\begin{itemize}
    \item USDA FoodData Central
    \item Indian Food Composition Tables (IFCT)
\end{itemize}

Across all trials:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Macronutrient estimation accuracy remained between 82--89\%
    \item Portion estimation accuracy ranged between 75--80\%
\end{itemize}

These results are significantly higher than manual logging,  
where portion inaccuracies commonly exceed 40--50\%.

\subsection*{3. Effectiveness of Personalized Recommendations}

The recommendation engine generated personalized feedback based on:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Daily calorie consumption
    \item Macronutrient balance
    \item Activity levels
    \item User goals (weight loss, muscle gain, maintenance)
\end{itemize}

Examples of context-aware recommendations include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item ``Your protein intake is low today — consider adding legumes or paneer for dinner.''
    \item ``You consumed excess oil-based foods — try including more fiber-rich items tomorrow.''
    \item ``Hydration levels are below average — drink at least 2--3 more glasses of water.''
\end{itemize}

The system's ability to generate specific, goal-aligned suggestions  
demonstrates the strength of incorporating generative AI models  
over predefined rule-based systems.

\subsection*{4. Real-World Usability and User Feedback}

User testing revealed several positive behavioral outcomes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Users logged meals more frequently because the process was automated.
    \item They reported better awareness of daily calorie and macro intake.
    \item The visual dashboard built using FlutterFlow improved engagement.
    \item Personalized recommendations increased user motivation to stay on track.
\end{itemize}

The overall user satisfaction rating was \textbf{8.6/10},  
indicating that the system was easy to understand, reliable,  
and useful for daily health monitoring.

\section*{Summary of Findings}

The results show that the Generative AI system provides:

\begin{itemize}
    \item High accuracy in food detection and nutrient estimation
    \item Automated and seamless calorie tracking
    \item Personalized, meaningful health recommendations
    \item Improved user engagement compared to manual methods
\end{itemize}

Overall, the system exhibits strong potential for large-scale use  
in health monitoring, diet planning, and lifestyle improvement applications.

\chapter{Testing and Validation}

To validate the reliability and robustness of the system,  
several test scenarios were executed covering various lighting conditions,  
meal types, user demographics, and interaction patterns.

\section{Testing Scenarios}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Single Food Item Recognition} – Images containing simple meals (e.g., roti, dosa).
    \item \textbf{Multi-item Plate Detection} – Complex meals with several items on one plate.

    \item \textbf{Backend Stress Test} – Multiple requests tested simultaneously via Supabase.

conditions such as low light or crowded plates.  
Backend operations such as Supabase database writes  
and API response handling remained stable with minimal latency.

User feedback validated the system’s practical usability,  
indicating that the Generative AI components greatly improved  
the ease of nutrition tracking and personalized health guidance.
